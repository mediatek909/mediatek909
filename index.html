BP 2010 Gulf of Mexico Disaster: A Systemic Failure

By Tuan, Gia Phat, Thanh Thao, and Thai Khang

1. The Sustainability Paradox

On April 20, 2010, the explosion of Deepwater Horizon shook the world. A significant environmental disaster that claimed the lives of 11 workers and polluted the Gulf of Mexico with approximately four million barrels of oil (EPA, 2025). By evaluating British Petroleum (BP) through the integrated lens of organizational sustainability and justice, this analysis argues that BP's systemic failure resulted from a greenwashed culture that prioritized short-term economic growth over environmental integrity and stakeholder fairness.

Organizational sustainability is defined as performance that meets the adequacy of present needs without negatively impacting future generations' ability to meet their own needs (Marcis et al., 2019). For a multinational enterprise to become fully sustainable, it must balance out the Triple Bottom Line (TBL): economic prosperity, social sustainability, and environmental integrity (Satar, 2022). Given three fundamental pillars, failing any one of them prevents long-term organizational development.

In 2000, British Petroleum rebranded itself as Beyond Petroleum (Macalister, 2000). An enormous, costly marketing campaign valued at $200 million to position the firm as an industry leader in renewable energy and corporate social responsibility (CSR) (Barrage et al., 2020). Nevertheless, this movement was a facade, or greenwashing, designed to mask an underlying reality of neglect. While BP claimed aspirational goals of no accidents and harm, their internal record of accidents noted 3,565 accidents between 1990 and 2003 (National COSH, 2006)

This created a sustainability gap where financial performance is detached from ethical accountability. BP's decision to reduce the safety budget to increase throughput is a serious violation of environmental protection requirements. By emphasizing short-term economic benefits over environmental safety, BP failed the core tenets of TBL, proving that a CSR report does not substitute for an honest culture of sustainability.

2. The Ethical Auditor: Leadership Failures

The collapse of BP's safety governance did not begin on April 20, 2010 it was built over years of deliberate structural neglect. Under CEO Lord Browne (1995–2007), BP's relentless "more for less" philosophy treated cost-cutting as a performance virtue. Between 2002 and 2010, BP reduced its workforce from 115,250 to 79,700 employees a 31% reduction, while slashing maintenance and safety budgets as executive compensation steadily rose (Bower, 2009, as cited in Nguyen, n.d.). Safety warnings from Alaska to Texas City were ignored, and the organisation accumulated 3,565 recorded accidents between 1990 and 2004 (Nguyen, n.d.). When Deepwater Horizon exploded, CEO Tony Hayward admitted BP was "making it up day to day", exposing seven cascading leadership failures from inadequate pre-event planning and poor crisis communication to media suppression and persistent dishonesty (Benoit, 1995; Coombs, 2007). Leventhal (1980) established six criteria for procedural justice, including ethicality and bias suppression both of which BP systematically violated. Safety decisions were filtered through financial targets rather than independent risk assessment, producing a structurally biased process that prioritised shareholder returns over worker safety (Colquitt, 2001). Unerman and O'Dwyer (2007) note that the 2005 Texas City explosion had already exposed the gap between BP's published sustainability commitments and its operational reality. No organisation can claim procedural fairness when cost-cutting is the primary bias distorting every safety decision made. The injustice extended to outcomes. Distributive justice requires rewards to be proportional to contribution (Leventhal, 1976), yet Lord Browne's compensation nearly doubled while thousands of workers were made redundant (Nguyen, n.d.). Mintzberg, Simon and Basu (2002) call this the "selfish syndrome" an ideology that glorifies the CEO-as-hero, incentivising reckless short-termism over genuine accountability. Hayward's crisis conduct dismissing the spill's severity and attending a yacht race as oil engulfed the Gulf was not a personal failing but the expression of an organisation that had never internalised responsibility to stakeholders beyond shareholders (Freeman, 1984; Hart & Milstein, 2003).

3. Stakeholder Integration Collapse

Beyond the "selfish syndrome" that prioritizes executive gains over collective safety, the systemic failure of BP is best illustrated by the "hub-and-spoke" model of stakeholder relations. According to Bowie, this model depicts the firm as a central hub connected to primary stakeholders, such as employees and financers, as well as secondary stakeholders, such as the media and government (i.e., other stakeholder groups) (Bowie, 2001). Although a sustainable organization requires "profound responsibility" and collaboration with these groups, BP's disaster showed a serious collapse in maintaining these crucial connections. The core of this failure is BP's unsuitable decision-making, treating the "strategic" connection (i.e., making profit) and the "moral" connection (i.e., helping the firm pursue other righteous actions related to the environment, social, etc.) unfairly. Goodpaster argued that strategic stakeholders are prioritized because of their capacity to affect the firm's profitability, while moral stakeholders are ignored (Goodpaster, 1991). By neglecting the environment and well-being of workers, critical stakeholders that should be embedded in any sustainable theory, BP focused exclusively on shareholders and investors. This narrow and "selfish" focus causes the collapse of the connection within groups, which the company depended on for its "continued and basic survival". Furthermore, the Gulf of Mexico (GOM) disaster highlights significant inequities in global accountability. While the US spill in 2010 led to immediate regulatory action, similar environmental devastations in developing countries are often ignored by capital markets. For decades, the Niger Delta has experienced higher annual pollution than the GOM, but responses there take weeks to action, compared to the minutes-fast reactions seen in the West (Ogbu et al., 2024). Similarly, Chevron's two-decade-long legal battle over toxic dumping in the Ecuadorian Amazon underscores how multinational companies often escape the "polluter pays" principle in non-Western jurisdictions (Percival, 2020). Finally, this systematic ignorance was enabled by an institutional failure within the US regulatory watchdog. The body responsible for offshore development was the same entity issuing environmental approvals, a clear conflict of interest that reflected administrative flaws found in less developed regimes. Consequently, BP's inability to integrate its external stakeholders equally caused an efficiency and legitimacy crisis that no amount of advertising could "wash clean".

4. The Future Strategist: UN SDGs

The ongoing problems in BP's leadership and its misleading image, pointed out in earlier studies of its "more for less" culture and $200 million spent on corporate social responsibility, come from a major misunderstanding: the idea that we can keep growing forever in a world with limited resources. Traditional business models claim that the economy can continuously grow, deliberately overlooking its nature as a limited subsystem of the global environment (Griggs et al., 2013). To effectuate fundamental reform, BP must clearly renounce the "selfish syndrome" (Mintzberg et al., 2002) and the reckless accumulation of capital. The organization must adopt the biologically and socially responsible principle of "Enough" (O'Neill, 2012). This principle posits that genuine organizational sustainability requires adherence to strict ecological limits, dismissing the intrinsic biases of distributive injustice that benefit executives at the expense of workers. Transitioning to the "Enough" paradigm necessitates that BP institutionalize the United Nations Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) as unwavering operational frameworks, surpassing the superficial marketing of the "Beyond Petroleum" era (Bebbington & Unerman, 2018). The mandate of SDG 14 (Life Below Water) must unequivocally take precedence over throughput targets. This guarantees the structural prevention of the catastrophic procedural negligence that resulted in the loss of 11 lives and the devastation of the Gulf of Mexico. Moreover, the vigorous integration of SDG 16 (Peace, Justice, and Strong Institutions) is essential for eliminating the systemic bias and procedural injustice that characterized Lord Browne's administration, particularly in relation to the environmental and safety failures that led to the Gulf of Mexico disaster. By adopting SDG 16, BP can ensure strict accountability within the organization, moving away from a focus on cutting costs to a governance system based on ethics, reducing bias, and genuinely caring for the environment.

Conclusion

Eventually, BP's catastrophic legacy illustrates that financial success devoid of ethical responsibility ultimately leads to failure. Only a holistic stakeholder approach that fundamentally incorporates unconditional justice and equity can achieve long-term sustainability (Donaldson & Preston, 1995). By avoiding greenwashing and prioritizing ethical stakeholders over profit-driven entities, organizations can cultivate authentic, enduring sustainable value (Hart & Milstein, 2003), which not only enhances their reputation but also contributes to long-term success and societal well-being.
