BP 2010 Gulf of Mexico Disaster: A Systemic Failure

By Tuan, Gia Phat, Thanh Thao, and Thai Khang

1. The Sustainability Paradox

On April 20, 2010, the explosion of Deepwater Horizon shook the world. A significant environmental disaster that claimed the lives of 11 workers and polluted the Gulf of Mexico with approximately four million barrels of oil (EPA, 2025). By evaluating British Petroleum (BP) through the integrated lens of organizational sustainability and justice, this analysis argues that BP's systemic failure resulted from a greenwashed culture that prioritized short-term economic growth over environmental integrity and stakeholder fairness. Organizational sustainability is defined as performance that meets the adequacy of present needs without negatively impacting future generations' ability to meet their own needs (Marcis et al., 2019). For a multinational enterprise to become fully sustainable, it must balance out the Triple Bottom Line (TBL): economic prosperity, social sustainability, and environmental integrity (Satar, 2022). Given three fundamental pillars, failing any one of them prevents long-term organizational development. In 2000, British Petroleum rebranded itself as Beyond Petroleum (Macalister, 2000). An enormous, costly marketing campaign valued at $200 million to position the firm as an industry leader in renewable energy and corporate social responsibility (CSR) (Barrage et al., 2020). Nevertheless, this movement was a facade, or greenwashing, designed to mask an underlying reality of neglect. While BP claimed aspirational goals of no accidents and harm, their internal record of accidents noted 3,565 accidents between 1990 and 2003 (National COSH, 2006) This created a sustainability gap where financial performance is detached from ethical accountability. BP's decision to reduce the safety budget to increase throughput is a serious violation of environmental protection requirements. By emphasizing short-term economic benefits over environmental safety, BP failed the core tenets of TBL, proving that a CSR report does not substitute for an honest culture of sustainability.

While BP’s external rebrand projected sustainability, the internal reality signaled a deeper ethical collapse.
